VoltMoto Begins — The Plan

Why I'm building a $4k electric motorbike that should cost $12k.

The Problem

I love electric motorcycles. I hate their prices.

The Zero FX is $12,500. The LiveWire S2 Del Mar is $15,500. Even the "budget" Ryvid Anthem is $8,995.

Meanwhile, the DIY EV community is building incredible bikes in their garages with off-the-shelf parts — QS Motors, Votol controllers, Samsung cells — for a fraction of the cost. The performance is there. The price gap is huge. Nobody's bridging it properly.

The Solution

VoltMoto. A high-performance electric motorbike built with proven, off-the-shelf components. Modular. DIY-friendly. Open-source BOM. And targeting $6,000 retail — half the price of the Zero FX with comparable real-world performance.

The Spec (Locked)

  • Battery: 20s12p (72V nominal, 50Ah) using 240× Samsung 40T cells
  • Motor: QS 138 70H/90H — 10-18kW peak, proven in DIY builds
  • Controller: Votol EM-200 — FOC sine wave, 500A peak
  • BOM Target: Under $4,000 for the prototype
  • Retail Target: $5,995 – $7,495

Why Not Build the Battery Myself?

I thought about it. A 20s12p pack at 540A continuous is a lot of energy — 3.6 kWh, enough to sustain a dangerous arc if something goes wrong. The spot welder for copper busbars alone costs $200-400. And if I mess up cell #237 out of 240, that's a $1,000 loss with no warranty.

After research, I'm going with EM3ev for a custom-built pack. Same cost as DIY (~$1,400), but warrantied, professionally assembled, and I can focus my time on the frame, wiring, and tuning.

The Plan

Donor frame conversion. Find a cheap used bike (Honda Grom, Ninja 250, or a dirt bike), strip everything, mount the electric drivetrain. This saves months vs. building a custom frame and gives me a VIN, suspension, wheels, and brakes out of the box. Custom frame comes in v2.

Why "Build in Public"?

Three reasons:

  1. Accountability. If I tell the internet I'm doing something, I have to do it.
  2. Community. The DIY EV community on Endless Sphere, Reddit, and YouTube has decades of collective knowledge. I'll learn faster with their input.
  3. Customers. By the time the prototype rolls, I want 200+ people on the waitlist ready to put down deposits. That validates demand before I spend anything on tooling.
